Insights Memo

Three findings worth a leadership conversation, from the HR dataset

🏠 Home
Summary: The live dashboard and Chart Builder tools let anyone explore this dataset interactively. This memo is the other half of that — the synthesized version of what I'd actually walk into a leadership review with: three findings, why they matter, and what I'd recommend doing about each one.

1Attrition is concentrated in two departments

Finding

Production (39.7%) and Software Engineering (36.4%) have attrition rates roughly double the next-highest department, while Executive Office sits at 0%. This isn't a company-wide retention problem — it's concentrated.

So What

Company-wide retention initiatives would spend effort on departments that don't need it. The underlying driver is likely specific to how these two departments are managed, compensated, or structured — not a general culture issue.

Recommendation

Prioritize a focused retention investigation in Production and Software Engineering before rolling out any broad, company-wide retention program.

2Attrition looks manager-driven, not just role-driven

Finding

Joining employees to their managers (see the Dashboard's "Attrition Rate by Manager" chart) shows teams of similar size (~21-22 people) and similar average engagement scores (~4.0) ranging from 27.3% to 61.9% attrition depending on who manages them.

So What

If attrition were purely explained by department, role, or even team engagement scores, similarly structured teams should land in a similar range. The spread suggests management practice itself is a meaningful, independent driver of attrition — not just downstream of department or team sentiment.

Recommendation

Audit management practices for the highest-attrition managers specifically, rather than assuming the fix lives at the department level.

3Hiring volume and hiring quality are inversely related across sources

Finding

Employee Referral (16.1% attrition) and Website (7.7%) retain far better than the highest-volume sourcing channels — Google Search (61.2%) and Diversity Job Fair (55.2%) — despite those channels producing more hires in absolute terms.

So What

Sourcing decisions optimized purely for hire volume or cost-per-hire are missing the bigger lever: retention. A channel that produces fewer hires but keeps them 3-4x longer is likely cheaper overall once replacement cost is factored in.

Recommendation

Shift sourcing budget and recruiter time toward Employee Referral, and evaluate Google Search / Diversity Job Fair pipelines specifically for what's driving early attrition — screening quality, role-fit expectations, or onboarding — rather than just funding more volume there.

4The raw pay gap by gender is mostly a department-mix effect, not a same-role gap

Finding

Company-wide, women average $67,787 vs. men's $70,629 (~4% raw gap). But broken out by department, the gap mostly disappears or flips: Sales and Software Engineering both pay women more on average, and the gap in Production and IT/IS narrows to 1-4%. The company-wide number is driven largely by department mix (e.g. men are a larger share of the highest-paying department, IT/IS).

So What

A single company-wide pay-gap number can be misleading in either direction — it can hide a real same-role gap inside a department, or manufacture the appearance of one that's actually a headcount mix effect. Neither the raw number nor "no gap" should be taken at face value without controlling for department (and ideally position/tenure) first.

Recommendation

Run pay-equity checks at the department (or position) level, not company-wide — and treat any department with a materially wider within-department gap than IT/IS's ~4% as the priority to investigate first. (Race-based comparisons in this dataset are directionally similar but limited by small sample sizes in several groups — see the Dashboard's "Average Salary by Race" chart, which excludes groups under 5 employees for that reason.)
